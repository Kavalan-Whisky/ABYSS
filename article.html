★ COVER STORY · 8 PAGES · BY LING PHƯƠNG

"The amp was
made of milk crates."

Six bands rebuilding the Da Nang doom-folk scene out of broken gear, banned amplifiers, and an internet connection that goes down for three days every week.

WORDS Ling Phương PHOTOS N. Trần DATE April 2026 READ ~15 min

The first thing you notice about a Vũ Bão show is that it starts thirty minutes late. The second thing is that the guitarist is twenty-two. The third thing is the noise. A wall of it. Six strings put through an amp built — visibly, on the floor next to the stage, with the casing off — out of milk crates, a car battery, and a speaker someone pulled out of a bus.

This is Bến Cũ, a converted ferry shed on the south bank of the Hàn river. On a Wednesday in March it holds about 80 people. The night I am here, in front of me, in a t-shirt with a hand-printed Vũ Bão logo, is a 14-year-old who has come on a moped from twenty kilometres outside the city. He is the only minor in the room. Nobody seems to care.

Da Nang is not a music city. Or it wasn't. For most of the 2010s the venues here booked Vietnamese pop covers and bachelor parties from Hanoi. The few rooms that tried to book anything else — Memento, Mưa Studio, Café 9pm — closed inside two years, usually because the rent went up, occasionally because a state inspector "found a problem". The scene, such as it was, lived on the internet, in WhatsApp groups, in cassette dubs of bands from elsewhere.

Then, slowly — first in 2021, then more loudly in 2024 — something started happening in the back rooms.

The first band, the first room

Vũ Bão means "storm". The name is also a complicated pun in Vietnamese — a homophone of "violent guarantee" or "guarantee of violence", depending on which dictionary you read. Their first show was in October 2023 in the back room of a phone repair shop in An Hải Đông district, on a stage made of three pallets stacked badly. The PA was a portable Bluetooth speaker, ostensibly for karaoke. They played for forty minutes. Nine people came.

The guitarist — Tuấn, the same one who built the milk-crate amp — was nineteen at the time. He had been playing along to Tibetan throat-singing recordings and Earth bootlegs on a $80 acoustic guitar with a contact mic taped to its body. His father, a fisherman, did not understand. The music was loud. It had no chorus. It went on for too long.

We were not trying to start a movement. We were trying to be loud. The first time someone called us "doom-folk" we had to look it up. — Tuấn, guitarist of Vũ Bão

By spring 2025 the scene had a name (doom-folk, vaguely — the bands themselves variously refuse and accept this label), a single venue (Bến Cũ, the ferry shed), one cassette label (Phù Sa Recordings, run from the back of a phone shop), and a small but dedicated audience that travelled to every show, often from outside the city.

It had no record label interest from outside Vietnam. It had no streaming presence to speak of — Vũ Bão have one song on Spotify, uploaded by someone they have never met, with under 4,000 plays. The average ticket price was 30,000 VND, or roughly the cost of a coffee. Half the audience paid in cash. The other half, the editor of Phù Sa told me, paid in homemade snacks.

What it sounds like

This is the hardest part of the article to write. "Doom-folk" is a placeholder, not a description. It implies metal heaviness applied to folk forms, which is true of maybe two of the six bands on the scene. The others fit it less well.

Vũ Bão is the loud one — sustained walls of detuned acoustic guitar through a homemade amp, with vocals that range from low-register chant to actual screaming, often in the same song. The lyrics, when there are lyrics, are in Vietnamese and reference 17th-century rural folk songs from central Vietnam — songs about flooding, about boats, about waiting for someone who is not coming back.

Cát Trắng ("white sand") is the quieter sister project, two members shared with Vũ Bão. Their music is sparser — a single guitar, sometimes a small drum, sometimes a single bell rung at irregular intervals. Their best song is eleven minutes long and consists almost entirely of one chord.

The other four bands sit somewhere between these poles. Sao Mai is the most melodic — actual songs, with verses and choruses, played at funeral tempos. Đường Lành has a violinist. No-Comma have only put out one cassette and refuse to be photographed.

The gear problem

One of the practical constraints shaping this scene is electronics. High-end guitar equipment is expensive in Vietnam in absolute terms, but also frequently impounded at customs. (The reasoning, when there is reasoning, is unclear — possibly a worry about modified speakers or shortwave radio interference.) Several of the bands have had pedalboards seized en route from Bangkok or Hanoi.

The result is a generation of bands building their own gear. Tuấn's amp is one example — milk crates, a 12-volt car battery, a Bluetooth audio module from a scrapyard, and a single 8-inch speaker. It runs for about four hours on a full charge. It distorts in a way that no commercial amplifier I have ever heard does. It also, when pushed beyond a certain volume, smells faintly of plastic.

This DIY constraint is, the bands will tell you, a feature. "We sound like nobody else because we cannot afford to sound like anybody else," says Linh, who plays drums in Sao Mai. "If we had the same pedal as Earth or as Sunn O))), maybe we would just sound like them. We can't. So we don't."

The cassette label

Phù Sa Recordings is the closest thing the scene has to an organisational centre. It is run by a woman named Hằng out of a small phone-repair shop in Hải Châu district. She does not consider herself a label owner. She considers herself, she told me, "the person who happens to have a tape duplicator".

The duplicator is a 1986 Sony model, salvaged from a karaoke bar. It dubs cassettes in real time, one at a time. In the past two years Hằng has put out 17 cassette releases, averaging 80 to 200 copies each, all sold either at shows or by post. There is no website. To buy a cassette, you message her WhatsApp number. She replies within three days, usually by sending a photo of which colours of tape are available.

Asked whether she would consider opening an online store, Hằng laughs for a long time, then says: "If I open an online store, then I have to ship to anyone, anywhere. Now, I only ship to people who knew to ask. That is better. That is what makes them mine."

If I open an online store, I have to ship to anyone, anywhere. Now, I only ship to people who knew to ask. That is better. That is what makes them mine. — Hằng, founder of Phù Sa Recordings

What happens next

The question that comes up in every interview I do for this article is some version of: "are you scared this gets too big?" Every band gives a different answer.

Tuấn says no, and he means no — he thinks small is the point, and that if the scene grew to a thousand people, he would probably not be part of it. Hằng says yes, mostly because she worries about the cost — if a foreign label signs Vũ Bão, she would lose her favourite band, but more importantly, she would lose the model that allows her to keep putting out tapes for the others.

Linh (of Sao Mai) says she is not scared. She points out that this scene already happened, several times, in several cities — Olympia in 1991, Lisbon in 2008, Bandung in 2015 — and that each time, the answer was the same: some of the bands signed and got bigger and forgot where they came from, and some of the bands stayed, and the scene continued, slightly less interesting, until the next group of teenagers showed up with a guitar and a milk crate.

She is probably right. But I wish I had stayed an extra week.

— END · DA NANG · APRIL 2026
