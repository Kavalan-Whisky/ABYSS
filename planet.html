Jupiter.

The largest object in the solar system that isn't the Sun. So massive that it does not orbit the Sun cleanly — instead, the two of them orbit a barycentre that sits just outside the Sun's surface. Jupiter is, in a sense, the only planet that pulls the Sun along with it.

The spot that has been there for centuries.

The Great Red Spot is a high-pressure storm system in Jupiter's southern hemisphere, big enough to swallow Earth whole — though it has been shrinking since the 19th century, when it was three times Earth's diameter. We have continuous observations of it going back to 1830, and possibly earlier (a "permanent spot" was noted by Cassini in 1665, although there is some debate about whether it is the same one).

The storm rotates anti-clockwise (when viewed from the north pole) and has wind speeds of around 430 km/h at its edge. It is held in place between two oppositely flowing jet streams — when those jet streams change, the storm changes. There is reasonable speculation that within a hundred years, it may dissipate entirely. There is also reasonable speculation that it has been re-forming in slightly different places for as long as Jupiter has existed.

Jupiter is a failed star — or, more precisely, a star that wasn't quite massive enough to be one. About 80 Jupiter masses are required to ignite hydrogen fusion. Jupiter has about 1 Jupiter mass. It is, by a very thin margin, a planet. — A. Wesley, planetary atmospheres lab, 2019

The four moons Galileo saw.

In January 1610, Galileo Galilei pointed his telescope at Jupiter and saw, over the course of three nights, four points of light moving alongside it. He realised they were orbiting Jupiter, not the Earth — and in doing so produced the first direct, observational evidence that not everything in the universe orbits us.

The four Galilean moons are, in order of distance from Jupiter:

Io · volcanic Europa · ocean Ganymede · largest moon Callisto · cratered

Io is the most volcanically active body in the solar system — its surface is being constantly resurfaced by sulphur eruptions. Europa has an ice crust over what we are now reasonably certain is a global liquid-water ocean — possibly the most promising place in the solar system to look for life. Ganymede is the largest moon in the system; it is larger than Mercury. Callisto is the most heavily cratered object known.

There are 91 other Jovian moons, most of them small (under 10 km diameter), most of them probably captured asteroids, most of them in retrograde orbits — they go around the wrong way.

A note on the missions.

We have visited Jupiter directly nine times. The first was Pioneer 10 in 1973 — a fly-by that revealed Jupiter's intense radiation belts, strong enough that subsequent missions had to plan for hardware survival. The Galileo orbiter (1995–2003) is still the most thorough survey we have done: it dropped a probe into the atmosphere and beamed back data for 58 minutes before it was crushed by the pressure.

Juno, currently in orbit, has been mapping Jupiter's magnetic field and interior structure since 2016. The European Space Agency's JUICE mission, launched in 2023, is on its way to study the icy moons — it will arrive in 2031. NASA's Europa Clipper, launched in 2024, will reach Europa around 2030 and conduct close fly-bys.

There is no plan to land anything on Jupiter, because there is nothing to land on. Jupiter has no solid surface. If you dropped a probe in, it would compress until it became part of the planet — somewhere down there, miles below the visible cloud deck, the pressure becomes high enough that hydrogen turns into a metallic liquid. That is what generates Jupiter's enormous magnetic field — the strongest of any planet — which extends millions of kilometres into space.

It is hard to overstate how strange a place Jupiter is. Most of what we know is from looking at the top centimetre of an atmosphere thousands of kilometres deep. Everything underneath is theory and inference. We have built, essentially, a single guess about what is down there. The guess is probably mostly right. The guess will, in time, be revised.
